Portfolio vs S&P 500 - We Believe Cash is King

A unique characteristic of the world across countries, cultures and history is that money or currency is the one thing everyone can agree upon for value.

We deem long-term free cashflow to be the preferred metric for estimating long-term desirability for returns. We also prefer to value our purchases based on price to cash flows.

We especially like the focus on cash flow and free cash flow as it is less suscepible to creative accountants and managements.

We believe that holding a core portfolio that is above average in the below metrics overtime and purchased at below average prices, should outperform in the long run

According to S&P Global and back-tested data, the S&P 500 Sector-Neutral FCF Index has significantly outperformed the S&P 500 since 1999 by an annualized 3.63% (see Exhibit 2).Here

We look for companies that can maintain free cash flows and grow them or distribute them to shareholders for as long as possible. If a portfolio company position is to degrade over time in its ability to earn free cash flows or returns on investment, we will direct the dividends or capital into our better positioned holdings to bring our average up. If our principle appears to be at risk of permanent impairment, we will sell and re-allocate the principle to our better positioned holdings.

We will especially sell or add to a position when it brings up the portfolio average without causing detriment to our overall portfolio duration risk profile.

If we are unable to identify superior allocation, we will put new capital to work into either the total market index or short term treasuries, depending on which has a greater current earnings yield.
